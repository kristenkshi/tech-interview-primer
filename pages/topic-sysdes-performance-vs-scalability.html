Performance

System performance refers to the measurement and evaluation of a computer system's efficiency and effectiveness in executing tasks and delivering results. It encompasses factors such as response time, throughput, resource utilization, and latency. A high-performing system indicates that it can handle a substantial workload, process requests quickly, and deliver outputs in a timely manner.

Scalability

System scalability, on the other hand, refers to the ability of a system to accommodate increased workload or growth without sacrificing performance. It measures how well a system can handle an increasing number of users, transactions, data volume, or other workload factors. A scalable system can gracefully adapt to changing demands by efficiently utilizing available resources and maintaining consistent performance levels.

Horizontal vs. Vertical Scaling

Achieving system scalability often involves scaling horizontally (adding more machines or nodes) or vertically (increasing resources on existing machines). Horizontal scalability is commonly associated with distributed systems, where workload is distributed across multiple machines or servers. Vertical scalability typically involves upgrading hardware components, such as processors, memory, or storage, to enhance the system's capacity.

Performance and Scalability Together

Both system performance and scalability are crucial considerations when designing and managing computer systems. A well-performing system with good scalability ensures that it can handle current and future workloads effectively, providing a satisfactory user experience while accommodating growth and changes in demand.
