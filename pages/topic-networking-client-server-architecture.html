Client-Server Architecture

Client-server architecture is a computing model where tasks and responsibilities are divided between two distinct entities: clients and servers. Clients are devices or software applications that request and consume resources or services from servers, while servers are systems or applications that provide resources, services, or data to clients upon their request.

In client-server architecture, clients and servers communicate over a network, such as the internet or a local area network (LAN), using various protocols and technologies. Clients initiate requests, and servers respond by processing the requests and sending the requested data or performing the requested actions, facilitating the exchange of information and services between the two entities.

Client

A client, in the context of client-server architecture, refers to a device or software application that initiates a request for a specific service or resource. Clients are typically end-user devices such as computers, smartphones, or tablets, or software applications running on these devices. They send requests to servers to obtain data, access services, or perform specific functions.

Server

A server, on the other hand, is a system or software application that responds to client requests by providing the requested resources, services, or data. Servers are designed to be highly available, scalable, and reliable to handle multiple client requests simultaneously. They often have more processing power, storage capacity, and specialized software or hardware configurations to efficiently fulfill client demands.
