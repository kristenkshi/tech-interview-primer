Consistency Patterns

Consistency patterns refer to approaches used in distributed systems to manage and maintain data consistency across multiple nodes or replicas. These patterns provide guidelines for handling updates, synchronization, and conflict resolution to ensure that data remains coherent and correct throughout the system.

Consistency patterns help system designers choose an appropriate trade-off between data consistency, availability, and performance based on specific requirements. The choice of consistency pattern depends on factors such as the nature of the application, the importance of data integrity, and the acceptable level of temporary inconsistencies or conflicts.

Weak Consistency

Weak consistency is a consistency pattern where different replicas may have different views of the data at any given time. In weak consistency, updates are not immediately propagated or synchronized across all replicas, allowing for potential inconsistencies and conflicts. This pattern offers high availability and scalability but sacrifices strong data integrity.

Eventual Consistency

Eventual consistency is a consistency pattern that guarantees that all replicas will eventually converge and agree on the same state, given that there are no further updates. It allows for temporary inconsistencies during the convergence process but ensures that the system will reach a consistent state eventually. Eventual consistency is often employed in distributed systems where high availability and partition tolerance are critical.

Strong Consistency

Strong consistency is a consistency pattern that ensures that all replicas have the same, up-to-date view of the data at all times. In a strongly consistent system, updates are synchronously propagated to all replicas, and read operations always return the latest written value. Strong consistency provides a strong guarantee of data correctness but may introduce higher latency and potential performance bottlenecks.
